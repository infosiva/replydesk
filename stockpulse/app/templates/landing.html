How to Read Stock Charts

A stock chart plots the price of a security over time, giving investors an at-a-glance view of past performance and market sentiment. The most common chart types are line charts (connecting closing prices) and candlestick charts (showing open, high, low, and close for each period). Candlestick charts are preferred by active traders because each "candle" reveals whether buyers or sellers dominated during that session.

Volume bars below the price panel indicate how many shares changed hands. A strong price move accompanied by high volume is generally considered more significant than the same move on light volume. Support and resistance levels — horizontal price zones where the stock has repeatedly reversed — help traders identify potential entry and exit points.

Understanding Technical Indicators

Technical indicators are mathematical calculations applied to price and volume data to help identify trends, momentum, and potential turning points. The Relative Strength Index (RSI) measures momentum on a 0–100 scale; readings above 70 suggest a stock may be overbought, while readings below 30 suggest it may be oversold.

The MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence) tracks the relationship between two exponential moving averages. When the MACD line crosses above its signal line, traders interpret this as a potential buy signal; a cross below is a potential sell signal. Bollinger Bands add standard-deviation envelopes around a 20-day moving average, highlighting periods of low volatility (narrow bands) that often precede large price moves.

No indicator is perfectly predictive — they work best when used together and in context with fundamental analysis. StockPulse combines real-time technical data with AI-powered fundamental insights so you can see the full picture before making any investment decision.
